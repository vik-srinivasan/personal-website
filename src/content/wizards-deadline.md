I wrote last summer that I loved what the Wizards were doing. That the rebuild was real, the direction was clear, and that for the first time since I was old enough to actually understand basketball, there was a reason to believe in Washington. I wrote about the young core, the draft capital, the OKC blueprint being executed in real time.

That was before this. And honestly, I still don't fully believe this happened.

I was sitting in the back of my algorithms lecture refreshing Twitter when the Trae Young news broke. I actually said "no way" out loud. The guy next to me thought something was wrong. Something was wrong -- my brain was short-circuiting. Because what the Wizards just pulled off at the trade deadline isn't just good asset management. This is the heist of the decade. I'm not being hyperbolic. Let me walk you through it, because if you haven't been paying attention to DC, you are genuinely missing the most exciting rebuild in the NBA right now.

## The Trae Young Trade

So. The Washington Wizards acquired Trae Young. A four-time All-Star. A 27-year-old elite point guard who has averaged 25+ points and 10+ assists in his prime. And the price? CJ McCollum and Corey Kispert.

That's it. That's the whole deal. No picks. No young players. CJ McCollum, a 33-year-old on an expiring contract who was brought in specifically to be flipped, and Corey Kispert, a solid shooter who was never going to be part of the long-term picture. Two guys who weren't in the Wizards' future for a player who could define it.

I keep replaying this in my head. Trae Young is 27. He fits the timeline of the young core perfectly. He's exactly the kind of star creator Washington has been missing -- a guy who can run a pick-and-roll, draw doubles, and create open looks for everyone around him. Trae feeding lobs to Alex Sarr. Trae drawing the defense and kicking to Bilal Coulibaly for open threes. Trae showing Bub Carrington what it looks like to command an offense at the highest level. I'm getting chills just typing this.

And the Wizards gave up nothing of consequence to get him. Nothing. The front office identified McCollum's expiring contract as the perfect salary-matching piece months ago, and they just... executed. Three steps ahead the whole time.

## The Anthony Davis Blockbuster

OK so if the Trae Young trade made me gasp in lecture, the AD deal made me leave the room entirely. A 9-player, 3-team mega-deal that landed one of the best big men in basketball in Washington.

What did it actually cost? Khris Middleton (expiring contract, brought in for this exact purpose), AJ Johnson, Malaki Branham, Marvin Bagley, two late or protected first-round picks, and three seconds. Go back and read that list. Every single one of those players was either an expiring veteran or a project who hadn't broken through. Not a single core young player was included. Zero.

Sarr? Still here. Coulibaly? Still here. Carrington? Still here. Tre Johnson? Still here. Kyshawn George? Still here. Will Riley? Still here.

The Wizards traded the margins of their roster for Anthony Davis and didn't touch the foundation. I've been watching this league long enough to know how hard that is to pull off in a deal of this magnitude.

And here's the part that makes me want to stand on a table: not a single one of the Wizards' own first-round picks was included. The picks that went out were acquired assets -- other teams' picks that Washington had stockpiled for exactly this kind of moment. Two years of accumulating draft capital as trade currency, and they just spent it on an All-NBA big man without mortgaging a single thing that actually matters. Will Dawkins, man. Will Dawkins.

## The Young Core Is Intact

This is the part that still doesn't feel real to me. After acquiring a four-time All-Star point guard AND a former MVP candidate center, the entire young core -- Sarr, Coulibaly, Carrington, Tre Johnson, Kyshawn George, Will Riley -- every single one of them is still on the roster. Still developing. The Wizards didn't sacrifice tomorrow for today. They found a way to have both.

My group chat with my friends from Wilson High has been on fire for three days straight. We grew up watching the Wizards break our hearts. The Wall-Beal era that never went anywhere. The post-Wall wasteland. We do not have the emotional framework for things to actually go right.

## Still Positioned for the 2026 Draft

Here's what pushes this from "great deadline" into "genuinely unprecedented" territory: the Wizards are 16-40. They're still one of the worst teams in the league. They're still positioned for a top-5 pick in what might be the most loaded draft class in a decade.

Darryn Peterson. AJ Dybantsa. Cameron Boozer. Any of those three could fall to Washington, and any of them would immediately become a franchise cornerstone alongside the existing core. The Wizards just added Trae Young and Anthony Davis and might still draft a generational talent this summer. I need someone to explain to me how that's possible.

And the draft capital war chest? Still massive. Twenty-six picks through 2032, including 10 first-rounders. They spent some assets to get AD, but they barely dented the stockpile. The cupboard isn't just full -- it's overflowing onto the floor.

## The Path Forward

There are two ways this plays out, and both of them are incredible for Washington.

One: Trae Young buys in. He loves playing with the young guys, he signs a long-term extension, and he becomes the veteran star this group rallies around. A backcourt of Trae Young and Tre Johnson, with Coulibaly on the wing and Sarr protecting the rim? That is a legitimately terrifying lineup. Add a top-5 pick this summer and you're looking at a playoff team within two years. I'm serious.

Two: Anthony Davis stays healthy and proves he still has elite-level play in him. The Wizards can either keep him for a push or flip him at a future deadline for even more assets. A healthy, productive AD has enormous trade value -- we're talking multiple first-rounders from a desperate contender. The optionality is absurd.

And the Sarr-Davis defensive pairing? Even for a few months, the rim protection from those two could be devastating. Sarr at 7'1" and Davis at 6'10", both elite shot-blockers who can switch onto guards -- I almost feel bad for opposing offenses. Almost.

## What the Experts Are Saying

Sports Illustrated graded the Wizards' deadline: A, A, A-, B+. They called it "the best deadline in the league." Not one of the best. THE best.

And honestly? They're right. No team in the NBA improved their short-term talent AND long-term trajectory the way Washington did. Most teams face a binary choice at the deadline: go all-in or stockpile for later. The Wizards somehow did both.

## The OKC Model, But Faster

I keep coming back to the OKC comparison because it's impossible to ignore. Sam Presti spent years in Oklahoma City stockpiling picks, developing young talent, absorbing bad contracts to extract assets. Everyone said he was crazy. Then seemingly overnight, the Thunder went from laughingstock to legitimate title contender.

The Wizards are running the same playbook, but faster. Will Dawkins, a Presti disciple, took those lessons and applied them with even more aggression. Instead of waiting five years for draft picks to mature, he's using the accumulated capital to trade for established stars at a discount -- players other teams need to move because of salary or timeline mismatches.

Trae Young was available because Atlanta needed to reset. Davis was available because the Lakers were imploding. The Wizards were positioned to pounce because they'd spent two years doing the boring, unglamorous work that nobody writes articles about.

This is what a masterclass rebuild looks like.

I'm a DC native. I grew up going to games at the Verizon Center with my dad, watching mediocre teams lose in the first round when they made the playoffs at all. I've suffered through two decades of Wizards basketball. I've defended this team to people at Stanford who literally laugh when I say I'm a Wizards fan.

Last summer I said I loved what the Wizards were doing. Now? Now I'm starting to believe they might actually pull this off. And for the first time in my life, I don't feel stupid for saying that out loud.
