Look, I'm not going to sugarcoat this. The Philadelphia 76ers just got bounced in the first round by the Knicks -- in six games -- and the Knicks didn't even have Julius Randle or a fully healthy OG Anunoby. I sat there watching Game 6, genuinely stunned that this team with "championship aspirations" couldn't get past a Knicks squad held together with duct tape and Jalen Brunson's sheer willpower. That loss still stings.

And what have the Knicks done since? Oh, just re-signed Anunoby and traded for Mikal Bridges. Cool. They might lose Isaiah Hartenstein in free agency and could move Randle or Mitchell Robinson for flexibility, but their core is terrifying. Meanwhile, the Celtics just won the whole thing and aren't losing anyone that matters -- Jrue, Derrick White, Jaylen Brown, Tatum, Porzingis, Horford. The East is a gauntlet, and the Sixers are standing at the bottom of it wondering what went wrong. Again.

But here's the thing -- Daryl Morey has been playing chess while everyone else plays checkers, and this is the summer it's all supposed to pay off. He didn't extend Tyrese Maxey last offseason. He shipped James Harden out in November. Every move was designed to create maximum cap flexibility for right now. The Sixers have a ton of cap space and tradable assets, and Morey needs to use them aggressively.

The urgency is real. Embiid just averaged 34.7 points in the regular season and 33.0 in the playoffs, both league-leading numbers. He also led all playoff players in on/off rating. Maxey won Most Improved Player. When both of them played, the Sixers went 29-7. That's a 66-win pace. But Embiid is 30 with a body that's been through wars. I've watched this man drag teams through the regular season only to break down in April and May too many times. The window isn't closing -- it's halfway shut already. Morey can't afford to whiff here.

So let's talk about what he should actually do.

## The Cap Situation (Bear With Me)

I know cap breakdowns aren't the most exciting reading, but you need to understand how much room the Sixers actually have to appreciate what's possible.

**Guaranteed money:**
- Joel Embiid ($51,415,938) -- yeah, that's a lot

**Non-guaranteed contracts:**
- Paul Reed ($7,723,000)
- Ricky Council IV ($1,891,857)

**Restricted Free Agents:**
- Tyrese Maxey ($8,486,620 qualifying offer / $13,031,760 cap hold) -- Bird rights

**Unrestricted Free Agents (basically everyone else):**
- Tobias Harris (Bird rights -- don't care, let him walk)
- Buddy Hield, Nicolas Batum, Robert Covington, De'Anthony Melton, Mo Bamba, Kyle Lowry, KJ Martin, Kelly Oubre, Cameron Payne, Terquavion Smith -- various Bird and Non-Bird rights

**Draft Picks:**
- No. 16 overall ($4,032,240 cap hold)
- No. 41 overall (no cap hold)

Here's the fun part. If the Sixers renounce all their free agents and waive Reed and Council's non-guaranteed deals, they can open up roughly $62 million in cap room. Trade away the first-round pick and that number climbs to nearly $65 million. That is an absurd amount of money to play with.

Now, Maxey is obviously getting his bag -- a five-year, $204.5 million extension. The kid earned it. But the Sixers won't ink that deal until they've used their cap space because they can go over the cap to extend him afterward. It's a technicality, but it's the whole reason Morey made Maxey wait a year. Smart, if a little cold-blooded.

Embiid and Maxey are a phenomenal foundation. Everything hinges on what Morey does with the rest of the money.

## The Third Star Hunt

Morey and the front office want to add a third star alongside Embiid and Maxey. Makes sense -- every contender needs that third guy. But this is where things get complicated, because the options range from "perfect but impossible" to "available but flawed."

**Paul George** is the name everyone's talking about, and honestly, he makes the most sense on paper. He's an unrestricted free agent, so Philly wouldn't have to give up assets. He fills the wing role that Tobias Harris was supposed to fill but never did, except PG is actually, you know, good. Eight-time All-Star, 22.6 points on 41.3% from three while launching eight threes a game. Plus he's an elite perimeter defender, which this team desperately needs.

But -- and this is a big but -- recent reports say the Sixers' interest has cooled. There's growing buzz that if George leaves the Clippers, it would be via sign-and-trade, which means Philly would have to give up assets anyway. He also wants a four-year max worth $212.2 million, which is a LOT of money for a 34-year-old with his injury history. I've seen PG disappear in too many playoff series to feel fully comfortable betting the franchise on him at this stage. That said, if he's willing to come outright in free agency? You do it and don't think twice.

**Jimmy Butler** is the nostalgia play. Jimmy in Philly during 2018-19 was electric -- I still think about that Game 7 buzzer-beater series against the Raptors and what could have been if the ball had bounced differently on Kawhi's shot. There's clearly a connection with Embiid, and Butler has been a monster in the playoffs throughout his career. But he's 34, injury-prone, and only took 2.4 threes per game last season. You put him next to Embiid and Maxey and suddenly the spacing gets really cramped. I love Jimmy's toughness, but I'm not sure the fit actually works in 2024.

**LeBron and KD** -- I'm mentioning these because every Sixers fan has fantasized about it, but let's be real. LeBron isn't leaving LA, especially if the Lakers draft Bronny. And while the Suns might blow it up after that failed Durant-Booker-Beal experiment, there's no way Morey wins a KD bidding war. Houston, Dallas, Orlando -- they all have way better trade packages than Philly. KD next to Embiid and Maxey would be absurd, but it's a pipe dream.

Here's where I think the conversation gets more interesting, though.

**Lauri Markkanen** might be the best realistic fit in the entire league next to Embiid. He spaces the floor, plays defense, and at 27, he actually fits the timeline. After trading for him, the Sixers would still have around $45 million in cap space to fill out the roster. The problem? Danny Ainge is going to demand a king's ransom, and after the Knicks just gave up five first-round picks for Bridges, every team with a tradeable star just raised their price. Getting Markkanen feels like a long shot, but I'd absolutely love it.

**Zach LaVine** -- and I cannot stress this enough -- I don't want LaVine anywhere near this team. Yes, the Bulls trading Caruso to the Thunder signals a rebuild, and yes, LaVine can score. But his contract is an anchor, his defense is nonexistent, and putting him next to Maxey creates one of the worst defensive backcourts in the league. He's a volume scorer who needs the ball, which is the exact opposite of what you want next to two guys who already need touches. Hard pass.

**Brandon Ingram** honestly feels like the most likely outcome right now. Reports suggest the Pelicans want to build around Zion, Herb Jones, and Trey Murphy, which would make Ingram available. I know a lot of Philly fans are worried this is just Tobias Harris 2.0 -- a mid-range-heavy wing who doesn't space the floor and plays meh defense -- and that concern is valid. But Ingram is only 26, he's already an All-Star, and there's a real chance Nick Nurse could unlock more from him, particularly from three. The trade cost would probably be lower than the other options, and at $36 million next year, the Sixers would still have cap space to add quality role players. He's not the sexy pick, but he might be the smart one.

One thing I keep coming back to: look at what the recent champions have in common. Denver and Boston both had deep rosters with great role players. The superteam model of three max guys surrounded by minimum-contract ring chasers feels outdated. Morey should keep that in mind. Sometimes the best move isn't the biggest name -- it's building the most complete team.

## Who Stays, Who Goes

Let's start with the obvious. Tobias Harris is walking, and I could not be happier about it. That max contract from 2019 has been one of the worst deals in recent NBA history. Tobias was supposed to be the third star, and instead he gave us zero points on two shot attempts in an elimination game against the Knicks. Zero. Points. In a closeout game. I'm not even mad anymore; I'm just relieved it's over. Goodbye, Tobias. Genuinely wish you well, but please never come back.

Now for the guys who should come back:

**Nicolas Batum** -- Aging, sure, but he was legitimately great last year. Smart defender, solid playmaker from the wing spot. He might cost $8-10 million a year, which is more than a minimum, but he's worth it. Bring him back.

**Kyle Lowry** -- This one is easy. Lowry is from Philly, went to Villanova, won a championship with Nick Nurse, and provides exactly the kind of veteran toughness this team needs. He's a minimum-salary signing at this point in his career, and he'd be perfect as a locker room presence and spot-minute guy.

**Kelly Oubre** -- This is the most interesting of the three. Oubre had a genuinely great season, averaging 15.4 points per game and bringing real energy whether he started or came off the bench. He seems to love it in Philly, and the fans love him back. If the Sixers could get him on the standard mid-level exception ($12.9 million), that's a steal. But he might get more on the open market, and I'd hate to see Morey get into a bidding war for him.

**Buddy Hield** -- Meh. He was disappointing after the trade deadline acquisition. Supposed to be this knockdown shooter, but he was streaky and inconsistent. If he wants to come back cheap, fine, but I'm not losing sleep if he signs elsewhere. His production is replaceable.

**De'Anthony Melton** -- This one hurts. Melton is the perfect backcourt complement to Maxey: tough perimeter defender who can knock down open threes. But he was hurt basically all of last season, and recent reports say he might command $10-20 million annually. That's way too much for a guy with serious durability questions. I love the player, but you can't pay that price when health is your biggest concern.

As for the non-guaranteed guys: **Paul Reed** has been underwhelming as Embiid's backup for years now. He looked genuinely unplayable in the playoffs at times. The Sixers could either cut him or keep his $7.7 million salary as a trade chip. Either way, he shouldn't be counting on rotation minutes next year.

**Ricky Council IV**, though? Keep him. At $1.9 million, he's dirt cheap, and he showed real flashes as an undrafted rookie. With all the roster turnover coming, there will be minutes available, and Council has earned a shot at them.

## Filling Out the Roster

Who the Sixers sign as role players depends entirely on which third star they land, because that determines how much cap space is left over. But let's talk about the types of players they should be targeting.

**Backcourt:** The dream addition next to Maxey is Kentavious Caldwell-Pope. He's a lights-out three-point shooter, a lockdown perimeter defender, and he doesn't need the ball. That's the ideal profile. If KCP is too expensive, players like Klay Thompson (if he's willing to take less), Gary Trent Jr., Immanuel Quickley, and Bruce Brown could fill similar roles with more trade-offs.

**Wings:** This is the biggest need. I've been really high on Naji Marshall -- he brings physicality and defensive toughness, and if his three-point shot comes along, he could be a real find. Caleb Martin is another guy I'd love to see in Philly; he proved himself in Miami's Finals runs. Patrick Williams, Kyle Anderson, and Malik Beasley are all solid options who could provide versatile minutes.

**Backup Center:** This might actually be the most important depth signing. The minutes without Embiid have been a black hole for years, and finding someone competent to hold down the fort is critical. Andre Drummond is the name that keeps coming up -- he was the best backup center Embiid has had, and a reunion at a reasonable price makes a lot of sense. Kevon Looney is another option. Jonas Valanciunas would be awesome but might be too pricey.

**Bench depth:** Guys like Tyus Jones, Monte Morris, or Kris Dunn could provide steady minutes in the backcourt. On the wing, Torrey Craig, Taurean Prince, Marcus Morris, and Jae Crowder are the kind of experienced, low-cost veterans who can give you 12-15 solid minutes a night.

**Trade targets worth watching:** The Nets just blew things up by trading Bridges, and they're probably looking to move Dorian Finney-Smith and Cameron Johnson for picks. Either one would be a great wing addition -- Johnson is an elite shooter (though he's been hurt a lot), and Finney-Smith is one of the most versatile defenders in the league. The Wizards are in full rebuild mode too, and Kyle Kuzma could be interesting as a secondary scoring option. Deni Avdija would be a slam dunk but Washington probably keeps him. Corey Kispert is a sneaky-good off-ball shooter. Portland could also be a trade partner -- Malcolm Brogdon's backcourt experience or Jerami Grant's athleticism on the wing could both help.

**The Draft:** The Sixers hold picks 16 and 41. Most people expect them to trade these for win-now players, and that might be the right call. But if they keep them, there are guys I'm genuinely excited about. At 16, Jared McCain out of Duke is a sharpshooter who could space the floor immediately. Tristan Da Silva is a 23-year-old forward from Colorado who's got the build and shooting to contribute right away. Devin Carter and Rob Dillingham are intriguing guards if they fall.

At 41, I'm really high on Kevin McCullar from Kansas and Harrison Ingram from UNC. Both are physical, experienced forwards with the kind of glue-guy skills that championship teams always seem to have. Look at what Dereck Lively has done for Dallas this year, or what Christian Braun did for Denver. Hitting on late picks matters more than people think.

One last thing: no matter how Morey spends the cap space, the minimum-salary signings at the end of the roster cannot be an afterthought. The Suns signed eight minimum guys last summer and fewer than half of them cracked the rotation. That's how you end up with a top-heavy team that collapses when your stars sit. The back end of the roster matters, and getting those last few spots right could be the difference between a second-round exit and a real Finals run.

This is the offseason Morey has been building toward for over a year. Embiid's window is closing. Maxey is about to get paid. The cap space is there. If Morey nails this summer, the Sixers could legitimately be the best team in the East. If he doesn't, we're looking at another year of "what if" and another early playoff exit. No pressure, Daryl.
