## I've Been Hurt Before

Let me set the scene. It's 2017, I'm in the upper deck at Capital One Arena watching the Wizards blow a 17-point lead to the Celtics in Game 7 of the Eastern Conference Semis. My dad turns to me and says, "Next year." That was the last time this team made it past the first round. I was in middle school.

Since then, I've sat through the John Wall Achilles tear, the Russell Westbrook chaos experiment (we got gentleman's-swept in the first round and called it progress), and the Bradley Beal supermax -- a 5-year, $251 million deal with a full no-trade clause given to a 29-year-old who'd never made All-NBA First Team. Not a partial no-trade clause. A full veto. The only other player in the NBA with that kind of power was LeBron James. We gave LeBron-level leverage to a guy on a team going nowhere. That's the franchise I chose to love.

So when I tell you something has changed, understand the years of scar tissue behind that statement.

## Finally, Adults in the Room

In 2023, the Wizards hired Will Dawkins as GM -- a Sam Presti disciple and key architect of OKC's rebuild. In 2024, Brian Keefe took over as head coach. For the first time in my entire adult life as a fan, the Wizards have a coherent vision. No more overpaying middling vets to sneak into the play-in. No more half-measures. They're starting from scratch, and they're doing it right: stockpiling picks, developing young talent, and committing to patience.

It actually makes sense. I don't know what to do with that feeling.

## The Young Core

OK but here's the part that actually gets me excited. The Wizards haven't just tanked -- they've hit on their picks. The early returns are genuinely promising.

**The headliners:**

- **Alex Sarr** (20, 2024 #2 pick) -- The centerpiece. A 7'1" mobile big who already flashes dominant defense: switchability, rim protection, shot disruption. If he keeps developing, we're talking perennial DPOY candidate. He *chose* DC -- declined a workout with Atlanta at #1 because he wanted to be here.
- **Bilal Coulibaly** (20, 2023 #7) -- Already a defensive weapon with the wingspan and instincts to guard multiple positions. The offensive game is still cooking, but the defensive ceiling is absurd.
- **Tre Johnson** (19, 2025 #6) -- The most offensively gifted player on the roster. True three-level scorer with shot creation and footwork that make you lean forward in your seat. Potential offensive superstar.
- **Bub Carrington** (19, 2024 #14) -- Tall, fluid point guard with advanced vision. Being groomed as the long-term lead guard, and the front office is letting him learn through real NBA reps, mistakes and all.
- **Cam Whitmore** (21, acquired from Houston for two second-rounders) -- A walking bucket. Strength, burst, scoring mentality -- real star upside. Getting him for two seconds might be the savviest move this front office has made.

**The supporting cast with upside:** AJ Johnson (explosive guard, acquired by flipping Kuzma), Kyshawn George (3-and-D wing with a clean stroke), Will Riley (raw but tantalizing 19-year-old athlete), Dillon Jones (jack-of-all-trades forward grabbed from OKC for essentially nothing), and Tristan Vukcevic (stretch big who spaces the floor next to Sarr).

That's 7 first-round picks from just the last two drafts on the roster, and 11 from the last four when you include trade acquisitions. That kind of youth density isn't accidental -- it's a team betting that if you swing enough times, you're going to connect on something special.

## The Vets Are Here for a Reason

This is where the front office has been genuinely clever. They brought in CJ McCollum, Marcus Smart, and Khris Middleton -- not to chase wins, but to set a standard. These guys are former All-Stars, champions, respected locker room voices. CJ gives young guards a blueprint for professional scoring. Smart brings championship pedigree and teaches defensive intensity by example. Middleton adds polish and shot-selection wisdom.

Here's the kicker: all three are on expiring contracts. Maximum flexibility. They're valuable trade chips at the deadline or clean exits off the books next summer. Same playbook they used to flip Kyle Kuzma into AJ Johnson -- move veterans at peak value, create space, add assets.

The Jordan Poole-for-McCollum swap was a masterclass. Poole had two more guaranteed years, didn't fit the timeline, and was soaking up shots the young guys needed. By flipping him, Washington got an expiring deal, a respected vet, and draft compensation. Clear the books, improve the culture, add to the war chest. Textbook.

## The Draft Capital Stockpile

Here's where I start letting myself dream, and I know that's dangerous for a Wizards fan.

Washington's 2026 first-round pick is top-8 protected, and the 2026 draft class is shaping up to be generational. Darryn Peterson, AJ Dybantsa, Cameron Boozer -- any of them would immediately become the number one option in DC and change the trajectory of the franchise. Even beyond that trio, prospects like Nate Ament, Mikel Brown, and Jayden Quaintance carry All-Star upside. The incentive is clear: stay in the top 8 and give yourself a shot at a cornerstone.

They've also got a second first-rounder in 2026 via a pick swap (worst of Houston, OKC, or the Clippers), plus eight second-rounders over the next two drafts. They've already shown they'll use those seconds creatively -- the Whitmore trade being Exhibit A.

The timing is almost poetic. McCollum, Smart, and Middleton all come off the books after this season, giving Washington max cap flexibility right as a potential franchise player arrives from the 2026 draft. By then, the young core will have multiple full seasons of development. The 2026-27 season is when this stops being theoretical. That's the inflection point.

## Tanking With Purpose

I'll be honest: watching the Wizards lose 60 games isn't fun. But for the first time, the losing feels *intentional* rather than incompetent, and that distinction matters more than you'd think.

Carrington is learning point guard duties the hard way -- through reps, mistakes, and real responsibility. Sarr is anchoring the defense with no safety net, battling bigger bodies every night with a razor-thin frontcourt behind him. That thin big man rotation? Probably not an accident. Fewer rebounds, more defensive breakdowns, more losses -- but also more developmental reps for young players being challenged beyond what they're ready for. That's how you accelerate growth.

This is the OKC and Houston model: prioritize experience over efficiency, let your young core fail, and live with the results. Don't chase wins that don't mean anything. Chase reps.

## Something's Different This Time

I know how this sounds. I know Wizards fans have talked themselves into hope before. But some things are harder to fake.

Sarr declined Atlanta's workout to come here. Cam Whitmore, a DMV kid, worked behind the scenes to get traded home from Houston. Ace Bailey reportedly wanted to land in DC and refused workouts with teams drafting above us. Players are choosing Washington. When's the last time that happened?

The organization invested in keeping the team in the city, with plans to revamp Capital One Arena and build a new practice facility. At Summer League, they built a private practice court in a Las Vegas ballroom -- the kind of detail that signals to young players that development actually matters here. Marcus Smart and Coulibaly showed up to Vegas to support the Summer League guys despite not being on the roster. That stuff doesn't happen by accident.

After years of lifeless basketball and empty seats, there's actual energy around this team. Not because we're good -- we're terrible -- but because for the first time, the losing has a destination.

## Letting Myself Believe

I've been a Wizards fan long enough to know this could all fall apart. Prospects bust. Lottery balls bounce wrong. Growth isn't linear. I watched this franchise give a quarter-billion dollars to the wrong guy three years ago. I'm not naive.

But I've also never felt like this about the team. Not during the Arenas years when everything felt like it could explode at any moment (and eventually did, literally, in a locker room). Not during Wall and Beal's peak. Not during the Poole/Kuzma era of expensive irrelevance.

What's different now is that the optimism is earned. There's a real front office. A real plan. A young core that makes you want to watch even when they're losing by 20. And a timeline that actually adds up.

It won't happen overnight. But for the first time in my life as a Wizards fan, I'm not bracing for the next disaster. I'm looking forward to what comes next. And honestly? That terrifies me more than the losing ever did.
